How to use this

Two minutes before you start.

Harshit — this isn't a syllabus to finish or a checklist to feel guilty about. It's a direction: the most useful thing in here is the shape of the next three years — what to front-load, what to ignore, what to keep returning to. Three ground rules:

How to read it

1. Trust the arrows, not the specifics. Tool names (today it's Cursor, Claude Code, this-or-that database) will be outdated by the time you graduate. The categories — fundamentals, building, AI fluency, judgment — won't be. When a tool name looks old, replace it; keep the idea.

2. Build > consume. A finished, deployed, slightly-broken project teaches more than ten perfect tutorials. Bias every week toward shipping something small.

3. It's about how to train, not where the jobs will be. The field moves too fast to predict the 2029 market — so this guide spends its pages on the durable skills that make you a good engineer in an AI world, and leaves the hiring forecasts for when they're close enough to matter.

01

The one big idea

If you forget everything else, keep this.
AI commoditizes typing code. It does not commoditize judgment.

For decades, "can you write working code" was the bar to clear to get hired. AI tools now clear that bar for almost anyone. That sounds like bad news, and for some people it is — but only for the ones whose entire value was producing code.

Here's the uncomfortable, useful truth: AI didn't replace the junior developer — it exposed the junior who never learned the fundamentals. In one controlled study, students who used AI to generate code scored under 40% on understanding it later; students who used the same AI to explain concepts to them scored 65%+. Same tool. Opposite outcomes. The difference was whether they were building a brain or renting one.

The goal, in one line

Become the engineer who supervises the AI — who can tell when its output is wrong, debug it, design the system around it, and decide what's worth building — not the one the AI supervises. Everything else in this guide exists to build that one thing AI can't hand you: earned judgment.

02

What's worth learning

Where to actually spend your hours.

Not all skills age at the same rate. Spend your hours on the right-hand column — the left isn't useless, it's just no longer where your advantage comes from, because AI does it for free now.

Becoming commoditized

  • Boilerplate & tutorial-level coding
  • Memorized algorithm patterns
  • Rote syntax recall
  • "I can build a basic CRUD app"
  • Résumé keywords & certificates

Staying — or rising — in value

  • Deep CS: OS, networks, databases, concurrency
  • Judgment on failure modes & edge cases
  • System design — architecting things that scale
  • Debugging & reading code you didn't write
  • AI-systems literacy (RAG, agents, vector search)
  • Communication & framing the right problem
The 70 / 30 rule

AI reliably handles about 70% of routine coding — boilerplate, common functions, first drafts. The final 30% — complex requirements, edge cases, correctness, the things that actually break in production — still needs a human. In practice, AI-written code often carries more subtle logic errors, so most developers now spend more time reviewing and debugging it, not less. Being excellent at that 30% is the whole game. Aim your learning there.

03

The five pillars

Your real curriculum — the one a CGPA won't capture.

Your instinct — Python, Git, problem-solving, web dev — is the right skeleton. Keep it. These five pillars are that skeleton, corrected and extended for the AI era. The order matters.

1

Fundamentals — deep, not surface

Real fluency in one language (Python is a fine start), plus the CS that AI can't fake for you: operating systems, networking, databases, how a computer actually runs your code. This is the difference between shipping fragile AI-generated code and knowing when it's wrong. Front-load this; it compounds for a decade.

pythonos · networks · dbmsfront-load
2

Build real things — deployed, in public

Your strength and your edge. Progressively bigger projects, actually deployed (not just running on localhost), living on a public GitHub. A builder's portfolio of working software beats a résumé every time.

Two rules to lock in early

Git on every project, from day one. Even throwaway scripts. Make commit-and-push a reflex, not a chore you relearn each time. (Section 04 is your refresher.)

Project zero: your own portfolio website. Start static (v1) to nail the build–deploy–Git loop — then grow it into something dynamic (v2): a blog you write in, or an AI chatbot trained on your own projects. Let the portfolio itself become your testing ground.

git everywheredeploy itpublic github
3

DSA & problem-solving — reframed, not dropped

Don't drop it, don't obsess over it. Memorized grinding is losing value — interviewers know it can be gamed. But algorithms-from-scratch still trains the clear thinking AI can't supply, and most strong engineering interviews still test it. The method: build features with AI while understanding every line, then periodically turn the AI off and write the key algorithms by hand.

thinking, not memorizinginterview-ready
4

AI-systems literacy — the new pillar

This is the highest-leverage skill on the whole list — and the one most of your peers will skip. Not "use ChatGPT" — actually understanding how to build with models: prompting and verifying output, retrieval (RAG), embeddings, vector databases, and agents / tool-calling. Going genuinely deep here, while everyone else stays at the surface, is your clearest edge.

highest leveragerag · embeddingsagents
5

The durable human "30%"

System design, writing good tests, methodical debugging, and plain communication. Employers increasingly rank problem-solving and communication above raw AI skills. Treat AI output the way you'd treat a teammate's pull request: useful, fast, and to be reviewed before you trust it.

system designtestingcommunication
04

Git & GitHub, hands-on

The ten commands you'll use 95% of the time.

Git saves your work in snapshots; GitHub is where those snapshots live so the world — and future-you — can see them. It feels fiddly for about a week, then becomes invisible. Don't memorize the list below; just keep it nearby until your hands know it.

The mental model

Your project lives in two places: the local repo on your laptop and the remote on GitHub. You commit to save a snapshot locally, push to send it up to GitHub, and pull to bring down anything new. That loop — change → add → commit → push — is 90% of Git.

Setup — once per machine / project

git config --global user.name "…"Tell Git who you are (stamped on every commit). Do the same with user.email.
git initStart tracking the current folder as a new Git project.
git clone <url>Copy an existing GitHub repo down to your machine.

The daily loop — you'll run these constantly

git statusWhat's changed, what's staged, what's not. Run it often — it's your compass.
git add <file> / git add .Stage changes for the next snapshot (. means everything).
git commit -m "message"Save a snapshot of staged changes, with a note on what you did.
git pushSend your commits up to GitHub.
git pullBring down commits made elsewhere (a teammate, or your other laptop).
git log --onelineA compact history of every commit — your project's story.

Branching — for trying things safely & collaborating

A branch is a parallel copy where you can experiment without breaking the working version.

git switch -c <name>Create a new branch and move onto it.
git switch <name>Hop between existing branches.
git merge <name>Pull another branch's work into the one you're on.

Undo — the "phew" commands

git diffSee exactly what you changed, line by line, before committing.
git restore <file>Throw away local changes to a file (back to last commit).
git restore --staged <file>Unstage a file you added by mistake.
git revert <commit>Safely undo an already-pushed commit by adding an opposite one.
The GitHub layer — beyond your own repo

A few words you'll meet the moment you touch someone else's code: a fork is your own copy of another person's repo. A pull request (PR) proposes your changes back to them and opens them for review — this is the heartbeat of open source and of every real team. Issues track bugs and tasks. Getting comfortable opening a small PR to an open-source project is one of the best signals you can build.

What a good commit looks like

Small and frequent beats huge and rare. Write messages that finish the sentence "This commit will…" — so git commit -m "add dark-mode toggle", not "stuff" or "final final v2". Future-you, reading the history at 2 a.m., will thank you.

The three-year arc

Term by term, now to 2029.

You just finished Year 1. The principle for what's left: front-load fundamentals → build breadth and find your lean → specialize and land a real internship → convert.

You are here Summer 2026 · the foundation sprint

The single biggest block of free time you'll get

  • Weeks 1–2: Python fluency + Git as a daily habit. Public GitHub live; commit everything.
  • Weeks 3–7: Build one real, deployed, public project you actually want to exist — your portfolio site is a great v1.
  • Make it a sampler: give it a web front (build), one AI feature like a RAG chatbot over your own notes (AI-systems), and one piece written low-level by hand (systems). Notice what you lose track of time doing — that's your data.
  • Light throughout: 2–3 algorithm problems a week, AI off, just to stay warm.
Year 2 · 2026–27 · builder muscle + breadth

Ship more, dig deeper, find your lean

  • Two or three progressively bigger shipped projects — each adds a layer: auth, a real database, an external API, a deploy pipeline.
  • Deepen fundamentals alongside coursework (DBMS, OS, networks are in your syllabus — align effort with grades).
  • Start AI-systems literacy properly: build something agentic; learn to verify and push back on the model.
  • First internship hunt — even a small or unpaid one. The goal is one real team experience before the big one.
Year 3 · 2027–28 · specialize + the big internship

Pick a direction and go deep

  • Choose a lean from what Years 1–2 revealed: product / full-stack, applied-AI / ML engineering, or systems / cloud / security.
  • One substantial portfolio project that looks like real work, not a tutorial.
  • The pre-final-year summer internship is the most important career event of the degree — it's the one that tends to convert into a full-time offer. Aim at a place that builds real product; treat everything before it as positioning.
  • Ramp up interview prep (DSA + system design) ahead of placement season.
Year 4 · 2028–29 · convert

Turn three years into an offer

  • Placement season opens early in the final year — interview prep peaks now.
  • Convert the internship to a full-time offer if you can; run off-campus and referral applications in parallel (a strong GitHub and open-source contributions are the currency here).
  • A capstone that doubles as your portfolio centerpiece.
07

Habits that keep you sharp

How to use AI without going soft.

AI can make you faster or it can quietly make you weaker — the difference is entirely in how you use it. Three habits worth building now:

Habit 01 · One AI-free day a week

Pick a day. Write from scratch, read the full error messages, use the actual documentation. This is the gym day that keeps the muscle from atrophying. Students who leaned fully on AI scored noticeably lower on understanding their own code.

Habit 02 · AI as a tutor, not a vending machine

Don't just ask it for the answer — ask it to explain the code line by line, to show you two alternatives and the trade-offs, to quiz you. The students who used AI this way outperformed. Same tool, used to build a brain instead of rent one.

Habit 03 · Debug it yourself first

When AI-generated code breaks — and it will — sit with the bug and find it yourself before asking the AI to fix it. Debugging other people's (and AI's) code is one of the most durable, hardest-to-automate skills you can own.

08

Your first week

Stop reading. Start tomorrow.

Momentum beats planning. Here's a concrete first week — small enough to actually finish, real enough to matter.

  • Create your GitHub account and make it public. Real photo, one-line bio — your storefront for the next decade. Then claim the Student Pack (Section 05).
  • Install Git; learn the daily loop. status, add, commit, push, pull — that's enough to start. Commit something today.
  • Spin up the v1 portfolio site. Even one page with your name. Deploy it free (GitHub Pages / Vercel) so it's live on the internet by Friday.
  • Push it to GitHub — and keep pushing. Every change, a commit. The goal this week is the loop, not the polish.
  • Solve two algorithm problems by hand. AI off. Easy ones. Just to feel the difference between reading and thinking.
  • Write down what you'd love to build. Anything. That note becomes your Weeks 3–7 project — the one that tells you what you actually enjoy.
